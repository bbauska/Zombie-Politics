  • Crop circles are usually found in cereal crops such as wheat, barley, or canola, but they can also appear in other crops such as corn, soybean, rapeseed, or sunflower.
  • Crop circles can vary in size from a few meters to hundreds of meters in diameter. The largest crop circle ever recorded was 800 meters long and 350 meters wide in Milk Hill, England in 2001.
  • Crop circles can have simple or complex designs, ranging from circles to ellipses to polygons to fractals to pictograms. Some crop circles have multiple layers or elements that form intricate patterns or symbols.
  • Crop circles can appear overnight or during the day, but they are usually discovered in the morning by farmers or aerial observers. Some crop circles have been witnessed being formed by mysterious lights or sounds in the sky.
  • Crop circles are not randomly distributed across the landscape but tend to cluster near roads, population centers, or cultural heritage sites such as Stonehenge or Avebury.
